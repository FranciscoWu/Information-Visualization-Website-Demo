Tracking Artistic Hubs in the 20th Century

View Project

About

This project explores how artists moved and interacted with cultural hubs during the 20th century by analyzing life events from the Getty ULAN dataset. Instead of focusing on where artists were born, we examine where they lived, worked, and traveled—uncovering the patterns that shaped global artistic networks.

In this project, we investigate the geographic dynamics of artistic activity throughout the 20th century (1900–2000), focusing on the mobility patterns of artists as captured in the Getty Union List of Artist Names (ULAN). Rather than treating birthplaces as the anchor of identity, we shift attention to life events—where artists lived, worked, exhibited, or relocated during their lifetimes. By mapping these movements, we aim to reveal key artistic hubs, the rise and decline of cultural centers, and the transnational flows that connected artists across regions and decades. This spatial-temporal analysis is made possible by querying structured data from the Getty ULAN SPARQL endpoint, enabling a rich, data-driven visualization of how the global art world evolved across the 20th century.

our working process

01. Data Preparation

We began by trying SPARQL queries on the Getty ULAN endpoint but got no usable results. So, we downloaded the ULAN XML dataset and parsed it using Python (lxml) to extract artist names, life events, places, and birth years.

02. Data Structuring

We filtered for 20th-century artists and built a dataset linking each artist to their activity locations. Birth years were used to estimate active decades, allowing us to analyze time-based trends.

03. Visualization

We created bar charts, maps, and heatmaps to visualize artistic hubs, how they changed over time, and how widely artists were distributed. Tools included pandas, folium, and seaborn.

04. Analysis

Findings showed shifting hubs (e.g., from Paris to New York), limited artist mobility, and growing global diversity. We also created a custom Global Reach Index to measure international spread per decade.

Key Visualizations

Top Artistic Hubs

This chart ranks the top 20 cities where artists were most frequently active during the 20th century, based on life events such as residence or work.

By parsing the Getty ULAN XML dataset, the team extracted all life event locations associated with artists. These were grouped by city and counted to generate a frequency-based bar chart.

Unsurprisingly, traditional artistic capitals like Paris, New York, Berlin, and Vienna lead the list. These cities served as global magnets for creative talent throughout the century, reaffirming their cultural importance in the international art landscape.

Global Distribution of Artists

This bubble map shows where artists were active globally, with each bubble representing a city and its artist count.

Each location from the ULAN dataset was geocoded using geopy and plotted with folium. The bubble size indicates the number of artists tied to each city.

The result highlights a concentration of activity in Europe and North America, while also revealing the emergence of other regions by mid-century — suggesting a growing global presence in the art world.

Shifting Hubs by Decade

This heatmap illustrates how different cities rose or declined in prominence over each decade from 1900 to 2000.

Since life events often lacked precise dates, the team estimated artist activity periods by assuming they were active between ages 20–70. Events were grouped by decade and city.

The visualization reveals a clear shift: early in the century, Paris and Berlin dominate. After WWII, New York and other global cities rise in prominence — reflecting cultural and political transitions.

Global Reach Over Time

This line chart tracks how many unique cities were active each decade, showing the growth of geographic diversity in artistic activity.

Artist events were grouped by decade, and the number of distinct cities was counted for each period to measure expansion over time.

The data shows steady growth — particularly after WWII — but also highlights a surprising fact: most artists were linked to only one city, pointing to either localism or incomplete mobility data in ULAN.

Continental Diversity

This histogram visualizes how many cities each artist was associated with, measuring individual mobility across the dataset.

For every artist, the number of unique cities mentioned in their life events was counted. The distribution was then plotted to show levels of movement.

The results are striking: around 87% of artists were recorded in only one city. This suggests limited mobility or potential gaps in the dataset, reminding us to interpret artist movement with care.

City-to-City Mobility

This custom index measures not only how many cities were involved each decade, but also how geographically dispersed they were across continents.

The Global Reach Index was calculated using: (Unique Cities × Number of Continents) ÷ Number of Artists. Each place was classified by continent and decade.

The index shows slow but steady growth in global spread. The 1980s–1990s were the most internationally diverse decades, revealing a delayed but significant globalization of artistic networks.

Featured Artists from the 20th Century

Here are six artists selected from the Getty ULAN dataset who were active during the 20th century. Their life events and movements help illustrate the artistic networks and geographic hubs that shaped modern art history.

Robert Rauschenberg

Lifespan: 1925–2008

Nationality: American

Profession: Painter, sculptor, photographer

Notable Works: Erased de Kooning Drawing, Retroactive I

Note: A pioneering figure in 20th-century American art, Rauschenberg was known for his “Combines” — artworks that incorporated everyday objects. He studied at Black Mountain College and was active in New York and other major art hubs.

Rammellzee

Lifespan: 1960–2010

Nationality: American

Profession: Multimedia artist, graffiti writer, musician

Notable Works: The Letter Racers, underground hip-hop and graffiti projects

Note: A key figure in New York’s 1980s underground scene, Rammellzee’s work fused science fiction, street art, and performance.

Mel Ramos

Lifespan: 1935–2018

Nationality: American

Profession: Painter

Notable Works: Lola Cola, Candy, Peek-A-Boo

Note: A representative of the Pop Art movement, known for blending pin-up figures with commercial imagery. Active in cities like Paris, Los Angeles, and Mexico City.

Barbara Kruger

Born: 1945

Nationality: American

Profession: Graphic designer, installation artist, photographer

Notable Works: Your Body is a Battleground, I Shop Therefore I Am

Note: Famous for black-and-white imagery with bold slogans in red and white. Her work explores identity, power, and gender. Based in New York and California.

Bruce Nauman

Born: 1941

Nationality: American

Profession: Sculptor, video and installation artist

Notable Works: Walking in an Exaggerated Manner..., Clown Torture

Note: A leading conceptual artist known for using the body, language, and space. Active in San Francisco and New Mexico.

Jean-Michel Basquiat

Lifespan: 1960–1988

Nationality: American

Profession: Graffiti artist, painter

Notable Works: Untitled (1981), Irony of Negro Policeman

Note: One of the most influential figures in street art and contemporary American painting. Known for his raw style and cultural critique, active primarily in New York City.

Discoveries

Through the data we collected and analyzed, we came to the following conclusions.

Top Artistic Hubs Identified

Using ULAN life event data, we ranked cities by how often artists lived or worked there. Paris, New York, Berlin, and Vienna topped the list, highlighting their long-standing influence as global creative centers in the 20th century.

Decade-by-Decade Shifts in Cultural Centers

By aligning artist life events with estimated active decades, we tracked how cultural hubs shifted. Paris dominated early in the century, while New York emerged as a powerhouse after World War II — a shift shaped by political and social transformations.

Artistic Activity Spread Globally Over Time

We saw a clear trend toward globalization. A line chart of active cities over time revealed increasing diversity in locations — reflecting how art scenes flourished beyond traditional Western hubs, especially in the late 20th century.

Global Reach Index

We developed a new metric to quantify how globally distributed artistic activity was. It combined the number of unique cities, continents, and artists — showing that while geographic diversity increased, most artists still stayed within one city.

Limited City-to-City Mobility

Surprisingly, 87% of artists in ULAN were associated with only one city. This suggests either a lack of movement or underreporting in the data — raising questions about how completely we can map mobility using existing metadata.

Continental Diversity Emerged Gradually

By mapping artist activity to continents, we saw that the dominance of Europe and North America faded slightly as Asia, Latin America, and Africa became more prominent in the global art network — especially from the 1970s onward.
